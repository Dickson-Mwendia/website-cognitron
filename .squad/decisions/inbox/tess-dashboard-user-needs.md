# Cognitron — Student Learning Dashboard: User Needs Document

**Author:** Tess (Parent & Student Advocate)
**Date:** 2025
**Status:** Proposal — Ready for review

---

## Executive Summary

This document defines what students, parents, and coaches need from Cognitron's learning dashboard — the core product experience that justifies our premium pricing and differentiates us from every other coding/chess provider in Nairobi. The dashboard is not just a feature; it is the proof of value. When a Karen mum opens her phone during a board meeting and sees her 8-year-old's chess rating climbing and their first Python project deployed, that's the moment Cognitron stops being "an extracurricular" and becomes "the best investment I've made in my child's future."

Everything in this document is grounded in how affluent Nairobi families actually behave — their devices, their communication habits, their anxieties, their aspirations. Generic EdTech advice does not apply here. These families compare Cognitron to ISK's enrichment programs, to what their cousins in London have access to, to what Stanford admissions officers look for. The dashboard must meet that bar.

## 1. Student Personas

### 1.1 Persona: Amani (Age 6) — "The Curious Explorer"

**Profile:**
- Year 2 at Braeburn School, Karen campus
- Starting Scratch + Chess (beginner tracks)
- Uses mum's iPad at home; has a kid-friendly Android tablet for school apps
- Loves Minecraft, YouTube Kids, and playing chess with her grandfather on weekends
- Attention span: 10–15 minutes per activity before needing a change
- Reads at an early level; relies heavily on visual cues, icons, and audio

**What motivates Amani:**
- Earning stickers, badges, and visual rewards ("I got a gold star!")
- Showing her parents what she made ("Mummy, look at my game!")
- Character progression — she wants a pet/avatar that grows as she learns
- Friendly competition with her small group of 4 ("I finished first!")
- Sounds and animations that celebrate her wins

**What frustrates Amani:**
- Too much text on screen — she can't read long instructions
- Waiting for things to load (Nairobi internet can lag on tablets)
- Not understanding what to do next — she needs clear visual guidance
- Feeling "stuck" with no way to ask for help
- Losing progress when the app closes or the tablet dies

**Dashboard needs:**
- **Large, colorful tile-based interface** — no dense menus; think Duolingo Kids, not Codecademy
- **Audio instructions and read-aloud** — every key instruction should have an optional voice narration
- **Avatar/pet system** — a character that levels up as she completes lessons (e.g., a chess knight that gains armor, a robot that learns new skills)
- **"Show Mummy" button** — one tap to generate a shareable card of what she built today (WhatsApp-optimized image)
- **Offline-capable activities** — chess puzzles and Scratch challenges should work without internet
- **Progress as a visual journey** — a map or path she's walking, not a percentage bar
- **Session memory** — the dashboard must resume exactly where she left off, even if the tablet rebooted
- **Maximum 3 taps** to get into her next activity from the home screen

### 1.2 Persona: Jabari (Age 10) — "The Multi-Track Achiever"

**Profile:**
- Year 6 at Brookhouse School, Runda campus
- Enrolled in all three tracks: Coding (transitioning from Scratch to Python), AI (ML basics), and Chess (intermediate, targeting FIDE rating)
- Has his own iPad (school-issued) and access to dad's old MacBook for coding
- Competitive by nature — tracks his chess.com rating obsessively, wants to enter Kenya Junior Championships
- Active in his school's coding club; has already made a basic Scratch game
- Socially motivated — wants to show off achievements to friends

**What motivates Jabari:**
- **Leaderboards and rankings** — he wants to be #1 in his cohort
- **Tangible milestones** — "I deployed my first website," "My FIDE rating hit 1200"
- **Cross-track connections** — he finds it cool when AI concepts help his chess analysis
- **Project ownership** — he wants to build things he can show his school friends
- **Tournament and hackathon eligibility** — unlocking access to competitions by completing tracks

**What frustrates Jabari:**
- Repeating things he already knows — he hates being "held back" by curriculum pacing
- Not being able to see how his tracks connect — "Why am I learning this?"
- Losing work because of a bad save or connectivity issue
- A dashboard that feels "babyish" — he's 10, not 6, and he knows the difference
- Waiting for his coach's feedback on a project for days

**Dashboard needs:**
- **Multi-track overview** — a home screen that shows progress across Coding, AI, and Chess in one view
- **Skill tree / progression map** per track — clear visualization of what's unlocked, what's next, what requires completion
- **Project portfolio** — a gallery of his completed projects (Scratch games, Python scripts, AI experiments) that he can share via link
- **Chess integration** — show his chess.com or Lichess rating, recent game results, puzzle streak, and upcoming tournament dates
- **Leaderboard** — within his cohort of 4, and optionally across all Cognitron students his age
- **Streak counter** — consecutive days of practice (chess puzzles, coding challenges)
- **Quick-launch coding environment** — one click to open a Scratch/Python editor linked to his current lesson
- **Coach feedback feed** — see written/audio comments from his coach on recent work, without waiting for a session
- **Achievement badges** that unlock real rewards — tournament entry, advanced projects, "mentor" status
- **Adaptive difficulty indicator** — show him when he's mastered a concept and is ready to level up

### 1.3 Persona: Wanjiku (Age 15) — "The Future Builder"

**Profile:**
- Year 11 at ISK, preparing for IB; already thinking about US/UK university applications
- Focused on Python + AI tracks; chess is recreational for her (plays occasionally with her brother Jabari)
- Has her own MacBook Pro and iPhone 15; prefers laptop for coding, phone for quick check-ins
- Deeply interested in AI ethics and wants to build projects with real social impact ("AI for Kenyan agriculture")
- Self-directed learner who reads documentation, watches YouTube tutorials, and has a GitHub account
- Values her time intensely — will not tolerate a patronizing or slow interface

**What motivates Wanjiku:**
- **Building a portfolio for university applications** — she needs tangible, demonstrable projects
- **Real-world relevance** — she wants to solve actual problems, not toy exercises
- **Mentorship from industry professionals** — she craves guidance from people who work in AI/ML
- **Recognition** — being featured in Cognitron showcases, guest-speaking at events
- **Independence** — she wants to choose her own project topics and pace

**What frustrates Wanjiku:**
- Childish UI — if it looks like a kids' app, she'll close it immediately
- Prescribed curriculum that doesn't accommodate her advanced level
- Not being able to export her work to GitHub or other professional platforms
- Lack of feedback on her AI projects from someone who actually understands ML
- No clear mapping between "what I'm learning here" and "what universities want to see"

**Dashboard needs:**
- **Clean, professional interface** — think Notion or Linear, not Duolingo. Minimal, typographic, no cartoon characters
- **Age-adaptive UI** — the dashboard should look and feel different for a 15-year-old than for a 6-year-old
- **Project workspace** — integrated code editor (or link to VS Code / Replit) with project files, version history, and deployment
- **Portfolio page** — a shareable, professional-looking portfolio of her best projects with descriptions, tech stack, and links
- **GitHub integration** — push code directly to her GitHub from the platform
- **AI learning path with branching** — she should be able to skip basics she already knows and go straight to prompt engineering or ML model building
- **University prep tracker** — map her completed projects to skills that universities value (AP CS, IB requirements, Stanford/MIT admissions criteria)
- **Coach/mentor messaging** — async text communication with her AI/coding mentor for project guidance
- **Certificate generation** — downloadable, verifiable certificates for completed modules that she can attach to applications
- **Resource library** — curated links to papers, tutorials, and tools relevant to her current learning path
- **Analytics on her own learning** — time spent, concepts mastered, areas for improvement (she's self-aware and wants this data)

## 2. Parent Personas

### 2.1 Persona: Dr. Wambui Kamau — "The Busy Professional"

**Profile:**
- Pediatric surgeon at Nairobi Hospital; husband is a partner at a Big 4 accounting firm
- Three children: Amani (6), Jabari (10), and Wanjiku (15) — all enrolled in Cognitron
- Lives in Karen; employs a house manager who handles logistics (driving kids, receiving coaches)
- Checks her phone between surgeries — has maybe 2 minutes to get an update
- Makes purchasing decisions fast when she sees value; cancels equally fast when she doesn't
- Currently pays KES 280,000/term across all three kids for Cognitron services

**Communication preferences:**
- **WhatsApp is primary** — she has WhatsApp Business on her phone; all her children's school communications come via WhatsApp
- Reads WhatsApp messages within 15 minutes; emails within 24 hours; never checks apps proactively
- Trusts voice notes from coaches — feels more personal than typed messages
- Wants a **weekly summary pushed to her**, not something she has to go looking for

**What she needs to feel her investment is worth it:**
- **Instant proof of progress** — "This week, Jabari's chess rating went from 1150 to 1185, and Amani completed her first Scratch game"
- **Outcome snapshots** — tangible artifacts: screenshots of projects, links to deployed sites, chess rating graphs
- **Coach accountability** — she wants to know that sessions happened on time, lasted the full 90 minutes, and covered what was promised
- **Sibling overview** — one screen showing all three children's progress without logging in separately for each
- **Term-end report** — a beautifully designed PDF she can share with family or include in school records
- **Comparison to goals** — "At the start of term, we set a goal of FIDE 1200 for Jabari. He's currently at 1185 — on track."
- **Safety confirmation** — she needs to know that the coach who visited her home today was the authorized person (especially since the house manager receives them)

**Dashboard needs:**
- **Multi-child parent dashboard** — tabs or cards for each child, with a rolled-up summary view
- **WhatsApp-native weekly digest** — automated message every Friday with each child's highlights (progress, achievements, what's coming next week)
- **Push notifications for milestones** — "Wanjiku just earned her AI Ethics certificate 🎉" delivered via WhatsApp or SMS
- **Session log with timestamps** — date, time, duration, coach name, topics covered, attendance confirmation
- **Goal tracking** — per-child, per-term goals set with the coach, with visual progress indicators
- **Financial overview** — payments made, upcoming invoices, package details (she delegates this to her husband but wants visibility)
- **One-tap communication with coach** — WhatsApp deep link or in-app messaging to each child's assigned coach
- **End-of-term report** — auto-generated PDF with progress summary, achievements, coach commentary, and next-term recommendations
- **Coach profile and verification** — photo, qualifications, background check status (paramount for home visits)

## 4. Nairobi-Specific Considerations

### 4.1 Device Preferences

Understanding what devices our students actually use is critical for design decisions:

| Age Group | Primary Device | Secondary Device | Design Implication |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 5–8 years | Parent's iPad or family Android tablet | Parent's phone (supervised) | **Touch-first design**; minimum 48px tap targets; no hover-dependent interactions; landscape and portrait support |
| 9–12 years | School-issued iPad (Brookhouse, ISK provide these) or personal tablet | Laptop for coding homework | **Tablet-optimized but laptop-capable**; coding activities need keyboard support; dual-layout design |
| 13–17 years | Personal laptop (MacBook or Chromebook, depending on school) | iPhone or Samsung Galaxy phone | **Desktop-first for coding/projects**; phone for quick check-ins and notifications; responsive but not tablet-primary |
| Parents | iPhone (60%) or Samsung Galaxy (40%) | Laptop for detailed review | **Mobile-first for parent dashboard**; all critical info accessible on phone; PDF export for detailed reports |

**Key finding:** At international schools, iPads are near-universal for under-12s. Brookhouse and ISK both have 1:1 iPad programs. But for actual coding work (especially Python/Web Dev), students 10+ need keyboard access. The dashboard must support both touch-first browsing and keyboard-driven coding.

**Affordable Android tablets are common as secondary/home devices** — do not design exclusively for Apple hardware. Test on Samsung Galaxy Tab A (the most common budget tablet in Kenyan households, even affluent ones keep these around for younger kids).

### 4.2 Internet Reliability

Even in affluent Nairobi neighborhoods, internet is inconsistent:

| Scenario | Reality | Design Response |
|----------|---------|----------------|
| **Home Wi-Fi (Karen, Runda, Lavington)** | Generally reliable (Safaricom Fibre, Zuku) but drops occur, especially during rain | Progressive loading; auto-save every 30 seconds; graceful degradation |
| **Boarding school (Peponi, St. Andrew's Turi)** | Shared Wi-Fi, heavily restricted, often slow during peak hours | **Offline mode essential**; downloadable content packs; sync when connected |
| **Mobile data (4G/LTE)** | Safaricom 4G coverage is good in Nairobi, but data is expensive (~KES 1,000 for 5GB) | **Data-saving mode**; compress images; avoid auto-playing video; show data usage estimate |
| **During home visits** | Coach uses family's Wi-Fi — reliability varies | Session content should be pre-downloadable by coach; activities work offline |
| **Car/transit** | Kids in school transport often use phones; connectivity patchy | Offline chess puzzles and coding challenges for "in the car" practice |

**Non-negotiable requirements:**
- All interactive content (chess puzzles, coding challenges, quizzes) must have an offline fallback
- Auto-save with conflict resolution when reconnecting
- Images lazy-loaded and compressed; thumbnails before full resolution
- Option to download a "weekly content pack" over Wi-Fi for offline use during the week
- Dashboard shell loads in <2 seconds even on 3G; content loads progressively
- Service Worker / PWA architecture strongly recommended

### 4.3 Cultural Expectations

What affluent Nairobi parents specifically expect from a premium education platform:

1. **Tangible proof of outcomes** — Kenyan parents, even affluent ones, are value-driven. They will ask "What can my child DO now that they couldn't before?" Every term. The dashboard must answer this question immediately upon login. This is not optional.

2. **Respect for the parent's role** — In Kenyan culture, the parent-coach relationship is hierarchical. Parents expect regular updates initiated BY the platform/coach, not to have to go searching. The coach should feel like they "report" to the parent, not the other way around. WhatsApp communication should feel deferential and informative.

3. **Security and propriety** — Nairobi parents are acutely aware of child safety. For online sessions: parents expect to be able to observe or drop in unannounced. For home visits: they expect ID verification, background checks, and a record of who was in their home and when. The dashboard must make safety visible, not buried in settings.

4. **Premium aesthetics = premium quality** — These parents are accustomed to polished experiences (they shop at Village Market, drive European cars, dine at premium restaurants). A dashboard that looks like a startup MVP will undermine trust. **The visual quality of the dashboard is a proxy for teaching quality in their minds.**

5. **Community and exclusivity** — There's social capital in being part of the "right" programs. Cognitron should feel exclusive but welcoming. Features like "Cognitron Family Events" and curated parent groups (think "Cognitron Parents Circle — Karen Chapter") resonate with this audience.

6. **Bilingual consideration** — While English is the primary language for international school families, some parents and younger children are more comfortable with Kiswahili. UI should be English-primary with Kiswahili as an optional language (nice-to-have for v2, but plan the architecture for it now).

7. **Academic calendar alignment** — Kenyan international schools follow a 3-term calendar (Sep–Dec, Jan–Mar, Apr–Jul, with variations). The dashboard MUST align with this — term-based progress, term-end reports, holiday program integration. Do not use a Northern Hemisphere academic calendar.

### 4.4 Safety & Safeguarding Features

Parents in Nairobi — especially for home visits and online sessions — have specific safety expectations:

| Feature | Priority | Implementation |
|---------|----------|---------------|
| **Coach verification badge** | P0 | Dashboard shows coach photo, full name, background check status ("Verified ✓"), and ID number (partial) |
| **Session check-in/check-out** | P0 | Timestamped start and end of each session; parent receives notification on both |

### 4.5 Communication Preferences

| Channel | Usage | Implementation Priority |
|---------|-------|----------------------|
| **WhatsApp** | Primary communication for everything — progress updates, schedule changes, coach messages, milestone celebrations | **P0** — WhatsApp Business API integration for automated weekly digests, session notifications, milestone alerts. Click-to-chat links for coach communication. WhatsApp-optimized share cards for achievements. |
| **SMS** | Fallback for critical notifications (session reminders, payment confirmations) when WhatsApp is undelivered | **P0** — Africa's Talking or Safaricom SMS API for critical alerts. Keep messages under 160 characters. |

### 6.3 Making Learning Feel Like a Game, Not Homework

**Core principle: Intrinsic motivation amplified by extrinsic rewards, not replaced by them.**

| Game Mechanic | Implementation | Why It Works for Cognitron |
|--------------|----------------|--------------------------|
| **Progress visualization** | Skill tree (like a game skill tree) where nodes unlock as concepts are mastered | Kids see a visual map of their journey; each node is a small win; "unlocking" the next node feels like leveling up |
| **Streaks** | Consecutive days of completing at least one activity (puzzle, challenge, lesson) | Streaks create habit loops; kids hate breaking streaks; Duolingo proved this works at scale |
| **XP system** | Points earned for completing activities; bonus XP for challenges, speed, and helping others | Unifies progress across tracks; a chess puzzle and a coding challenge both contribute to total XP |
| **Badges / achievements** | 50+ achievements across all tracks; some common ("First Login," "10-Day Streak"), some rare ("Tournament Winner," "Deployed First App") | Collection instinct; rare badges become status symbols within the Cognitron community |
| **Cohort leaderboard** | Weekly ranking within their group of 4 (opt-in, resettable) | Small-group competition is motivating without being crushing; everyone can realistically be #1 |
| **Seasonal events** | Monthly challenges tied to real-world events ("AI Art Month," "Chess Puzzle Marathon," "Kenya Independence Day Coding Challenge") | Creates urgency and novelty; prevents the platform from feeling stale |
| **Narrative / story progression** | For younger kids (5–9): a story-driven wrapper where completing lessons advances a plot | Learning feels like playing a game; each lesson is a "quest" with context |
| **"Show & Tell" moments** | After completing a project, the dashboard celebrates with confetti animation and generates a share card | The emotional peak of completing something is amplified; sharing it extends the reward |
| **Mystery rewards** | Occasional surprise rewards for consistent effort — unlockable themes, avatar items, real-world perks (e.g., "You've earned a free chess tournament entry!") | Unpredictability drives engagement; variable reward schedules are the most compelling (behavioral psychology) |
| **No punishment mechanics** | No lives, no hearts, no penalties for wrong answers; mistakes are reframed as "experiments" | Learning requires risk-taking; punishing mistakes creates anxiety, not curiosity |

**Critical guard rail:** Gamification must serve learning, not replace it. If a student is just grinding XP without understanding concepts, the system should detect this (e.g., repeated wrong answers on same topic = redirect to remedial content, not more XP opportunities). The coach must remain the authority on whether a student has truly mastered a concept, not the point system.

5. **Content delivery for offline** — plan the sync/cache strategy before building content
6. **Internationalization framework** — English-first, but i18n-ready for Kiswahili and potentially French (for regional expansion)
